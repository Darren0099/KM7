Basic Lines: The Foundation

Straight Lines

Try with different pressure

Start with your pencil at one point, breathe, and draw a steady line to the end point. Practice light, medium, and heavy pressure.

Curved Lines

Feel the flow

Allow your hand to move naturally in an arc. Don't force it—let your wrist and arm create the curve with gentle movement.

Hatching Techniques

Create texture and shadow

Parallel lines create texture and shadow. Vary the spacing and direction to create different effects and dimensions.

Dotting Technique

Patience creates beauty

Build up texture and tone gradually with dots. The closer together, the darker the area appears. Perfect for creating soft gradients.

Shapes & Composition

Simple Cat

Start with a circle, add triangular ears, then facial features. Simple shapes create character!

Coffee Cup

Begin with cylindrical shapes, add a handle, then details like steam. Ellipses create dimension.

Potted Plant

Draw the pot first, then add the stem and branching leaves. Curved lines create organic shapes.

Composition Tips

Rule of Thirds

Place key elements at the intersections of third-lines for balanced, interesting compositions.

Focal Point

Create a clear center of interest that draws the eye. Use contrast, detail, or positioning to emphasize it.

Balance & Space

Balance visual weight across your composition. Use white space to give elements room to breathe.

Drawing Daily Objects

Leaf

Start with the overall shape, add the main vein, then branch out with smaller veins.

Book

Begin with a rectangle, add perspective lines, then details like pages and cover text.

Bicycle

Start with two circles for wheels, connect with frame lines, then add details like handlebars and pedals.

Light Bulb

Draw the bulb shape first, add the base, then internal filament. Finish with light rays for effect.

Umbrella

Begin with the handle, add the top curve, then spokes. Fill in fabric sections with curved lines.

Drawing Tips

Observe First, Draw Second

Spend more time looking at your subject than at your paper. Notice shapes, proportions, and how light falls across surfaces.

Start with Basic Shapes

Break down complex objects into simple geometric forms: circles, squares, triangles. Build detail gradually on this foundation.

Use Light Guidelines

Begin with very light lines that can be adjusted or erased. Darken your lines only when you're confident with the placement.

Practice Regularly

Even 10 minutes daily builds skill faster than occasional long sessions. Keep a small sketchbook with you for quick practice.

Embrace Imperfection

Line drawings don't need to be photorealistic. Small quirks and variations add character and a human touch to your work.

Vary Your Line Weight

Use thicker lines for foreground elements and outlines, thinner lines for details and background elements.
